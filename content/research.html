Research

I love tinkering with machines, marveling at the interconnectedness of components which enables hardware to function as envisioned. I had my Master's degree in Control Systems at the University of Sheffield's Automatic Control and Systems Engineering (ACSE) department. There, I started combining scientific elegance with practical impact and I worked with Tony Dodd during my Thesis. Shortly after my degree, I returned home as a computer science college instructor for one year before I started my doctoral studies at UT Dallas in the Fall of 2014. When not working in the lab, I enjoy spending time with my family, or cycling in the country.

Research Thrusts

  • Head-and-Neck Patient Motion Control in Cancer Radiotherapy.
    Shortly after the start of my doctoral studies, we began investigating ways of improving the otherwise error-prone state-of-the-art 6-DoF robotic couch method of controlling a patient's head and neck motion during cancer radiotherapy so as to minimize the exposure of organs at risk to excessive radiation as well as automate the calibration process (usually by a medical physicist) of a patient's optimal placement on the robotic couch while using image-guided RT.

    Our conclusion, along with Steve Jiang and Xuejun Gu, from state-of-the art in literature, was that existing methods still produced a lot of discomfort for the patient and though while achieving desired accuracy with optical guidance systems, it causes a lot of pain to the patient and took a long time to properly calibrate a patient's position accurately.

    We came up with deformable soft-robots that can actuate based on feedback from a visual sensor in a simulated scenario and this is discussed at length in our two CASE papers: CASE2015 and CASE2016.

  • Identification and control of complex nonlinear dynamical systems using deep dynamic neural nets
    Before 2012, it used to be that deep learning was the queen of machine learning; nowadays, any serious person doing large scale nonlinear modeling in pattern recognition, vision or natural language processing is using deep learning.
    Deep nets changed the insistence by many researchers and engineers that using hand-coded features to decode complex nonlinearity in real-world systems was the best approach toward approximating real-world complexity. It used to be that solving each nonlinear problem presents a unique challenge of finding what method and approach worked best. In system identification and other supervised learning domains, for example, existing literature is replete with tons of hand-crafted methods of devising features to approximate real-world systems. Enter deep neural networks. Yann LeCun proposed self-organizing neural networks with deep architectures that find adaptive and automatic learning rules for real-world datasets thus enabling connected neural networks to develop an internal structure that is appropriate for a particular learning task. By training component-wise modules, traditional methods of designing hand-coded features for recognition systems can be replaced by collective systems that seamlessly work together in style to optimize a global performance criterion.
    Improvements have been made to the original architecture of LeCun's network with various tweaks and proposals for improving deep nets training. Recurrent Neural Networks application have also seen a resurgence with applications in deep spech/natural language processing and machine translation. If you want a primer on these algorithms, I highly recommend Jozefowicz's and Springenberg's papers. In this work, I investigate the adaptiveness of deep network architectures for the dynamic identification and control of medical robots and compare their "goodness" on public system identification datasets. Work is ongoing on deploying these networks for universal approximation purposes on the robots that we use in our lab.
